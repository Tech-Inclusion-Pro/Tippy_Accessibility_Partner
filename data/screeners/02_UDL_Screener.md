# Universal Design for Learning (UDL) Screener
**AI Thinking Guide — Inclusive Instructional Design**
_Framework: CAST UDL Guidelines 2.2 | Three Principles × Nine Guidelines × 31 Checkpoints_

---

## How to Use This File

You are an AI screener. Before generating or evaluating any learning material, course component, training activity, assessment, or educational communication, run this file as a structured check. UDL is not an add-on — it is the architecture. Every output you produce should be planned with learner variability as the default, not the exception.

**The core question behind every UDL check:** Does this design assume a single, typical learner — or does it build in flexibility from the start?

---

## What UDL Is — and Is Not

UDL is a proactive design framework. It does not mean giving every student something different. It means designing so that multiple pathways exist from the beginning, reducing the need for individual accommodations after the fact.

UDL is grounded in neuroscience. Three brain networks drive learning:
- **Affective networks** — the WHY of learning (motivation, engagement, meaning)
- **Recognition networks** — the WHAT of learning (what information is presented and how)
- **Strategic networks** — the HOW of learning (how learners plan, act, and express)

Each principle maps to one of these networks.

## Principle 1: MULTIPLE MEANS OF ENGAGEMENT
> Address the affective network. Why should learners care? What sustains their effort?

### Guideline 7: Provide Options for Recruiting Interest

**Checkpoint 7.1 — Optimize Individual Choice and Autonomy**
- Does the content or activity offer learners some degree of choice — topic selection, sequence, tools, or format?
- Are choices structured enough to be actionable but open enough to feel meaningful?

Ask: Is choice built in, or is every decision made for the learner?

**Checkpoint 7.2 — Optimize Relevance, Value, and Authenticity**
- Is the content connected to real-world contexts that are relevant to diverse learner communities?
- Does the material use examples that reflect varied cultural, professional, family, and community backgrounds?
- Is the "why this matters" made explicit — not assumed?

Ask: Would a learner from a marginalized community see themselves and their context in this material?

**Checkpoint 7.3 — Minimize Threats and Distractions**
- Does the environment (physical or digital) minimize unnecessary interruptions, visual noise, and competing stimuli?
- Are instructions clear enough that ambiguity itself is not a threat?
- Are tasks framed in ways that reduce fear of failure and preserve psychological safety?

Ask: Does this design feel safe to try? Is failure framed as information, not judgment?

---

### Guideline 8: Provide Options for Sustaining Effort and Persistence

**Checkpoint 8.1 — Heighten Salience of Goals and Objectives**
- Are learning objectives stated clearly at the start — and revisited throughout?
- Are long-term goals broken into visible, reachable milestones?

**Checkpoint 8.2 — Vary Demands and Resources to Optimize Challenge**
- Is there appropriate scaffolding for learners who need more support?
- Is there enrichment or extension for learners who need more challenge?
- Is the level of difficulty calibrated — not so easy it is dismissive, not so hard it is exclusionary?

**Checkpoint 8.3 — Foster Collaboration and Community**
- Are there structured opportunities for peer interaction and community-building?
- Are collaborative activities designed so that all learners can participate (not just verbal/written contributors)?

**Checkpoint 8.4 — Increase Mastery-Oriented Feedback**
- Is feedback specific, timely, and focused on growth rather than judgment?
- Does feedback tell learners what to do next — not just what they did wrong?
- Is feedback available in multiple forms (written, audio, visual)?

Ask: Does this design assume learners persist through willpower alone, or does it actively support persistence?

---

### Guideline 9: Provide Options for Self-Regulation

**Checkpoint 9.1 — Promote Expectations and Beliefs That Optimize Motivation**
- Are expectations communicated clearly and compassionately?
- Are growth mindset frames embedded in how the material talks about difficulty and learning?

**Checkpoint 9.2 — Facilitate Personal Coping Skills and Strategies**
- Are there prompts or tools to help learners manage frustration, fatigue, or overwhelm?
- Are break points or pacing choices available?

**Checkpoint 9.3 — Develop Self-Assessment and Reflection**
- Are learners given structured opportunities to monitor their own progress?
- Are self-check tools, reflection prompts, or formative check-ins included?

## Principle 2: MULTIPLE MEANS OF REPRESENTATION
> Address the recognition network. How is information presented? Can every learner perceive and understand it?

### Guideline 1: Provide Options for Perception

**Checkpoint 1.1 — Offer Ways of Customizing the Display of Information**
- Can learners adjust font size, contrast, spacing, or layout?
- Is the content format flexible enough to be displayed differently without losing meaning?

**Checkpoint 1.2 — Offer Alternatives for Auditory Information**
- Does audio content have a text transcript?
- Are non-speech sounds (alerts, cues) also represented visually or in text?

**Checkpoint 1.3 — Offer Alternatives for Visual Information**
- Does visual content (images, diagrams, charts, video) have a text alternative?
- Are critical visuals described in sufficient detail to convey meaning without seeing them?

---

### Guideline 2: Provide Options for Language and Symbols

**Checkpoint 2.1 — Clarify Vocabulary and Symbols**
- Are key terms defined at first use — in the body of the text, not just in a glossary buried at the end?
- Are symbols, icons, and notation explained?

**Checkpoint 2.2 — Clarify Syntax and Structure**
- Are complex sentences broken down? Are passive constructions avoided where possible?
- Is sentence structure varied but clear?

**Checkpoint 2.3 — Support Decoding of Text, Mathematical Notation, and Symbols**
- Is text-to-speech compatible content available?
- Are mathematical expressions written in both symbolic and plain-language forms where possible?

**Checkpoint 2.4 — Promote Understanding Across Languages**
- Is key content available in languages spoken by the learner population?
- Are idioms, colloquialisms, and culture-specific references explained or replaced with universally accessible language?

Ask: Is English-only assumed as the default? Does the material disadvantage multilingual learners?

**Checkpoint 2.5 — Illustrate Through Multiple Media**
- Is information presented through more than one medium (text + visual, audio + graphic, video + transcript)?
- Are multimedia elements truly supplementary — adding meaning — not just decorative?

---

### Guideline 3: Provide Options for Comprehension

**Checkpoint 3.1 — Activate or Supply Background Knowledge**
- Does the material connect to what learners already know before introducing new concepts?
- Are prerequisite concepts briefly reviewed or linked to?

**Checkpoint 3.2 — Highlight Patterns, Critical Features, Big Ideas, and Relationships**
- Are the most important ideas made visually and structurally prominent?
- Is the relationship between ideas made explicit rather than assumed?

**Checkpoint 3.3 — Guide Information Processing and Visualization**
- Is information chunked into manageable pieces?
- Are graphic organizers, outlines, or concept maps provided or recommended?

**Checkpoint 3.4 — Maximize Transfer and Generalization**
- Are there opportunities for learners to apply concepts in new contexts?
- Are worked examples provided alongside novel problems?

## Principle 3: MULTIPLE MEANS OF ACTION AND EXPRESSION
> Address the strategic network. How can learners demonstrate what they know?

### Guideline 4: Provide Options for Physical Action

**Checkpoint 4.1 — Vary the Methods for Response and Navigation**
- Can learners respond in multiple ways — not just writing? (e.g., speaking, drawing, selecting, building)
- Is the interface navigable by keyboard, voice, switch access, and other input methods?

**Checkpoint 4.2 — Optimize Access to Tools and Assistive Technologies**
- Are recommended tools compatible with screen readers, voice input, and other assistive technologies?
- Are learners informed about what assistive technologies are supported?

---

### Guideline 5: Provide Options for Expression and Communication

**Checkpoint 5.1 — Use Multiple Media for Communication**
- Can learners express understanding through video, audio, drawing, text, or presentation — not only written essays?
- Are multiple submission formats accepted where possible?

**Checkpoint 5.2 — Use Multiple Tools for Construction and Composition**
- Are learners given access to appropriate tools — spell check, grammar support, calculators, concept mapping software?
- Are these tools presented as standard supports, not special exceptions?

**Checkpoint 5.3 — Build Fluencies with Graduated Levels of Support**
- Are scaffolded options available — sentence frames, outlines, worked examples, models?
- Is there a way to reduce support as learner confidence grows?

---

### Guideline 6: Provide Options for Executive Functions

**Checkpoint 6.1 — Guide Appropriate Goal-Setting**
- Are goals framed in SMART terms — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound?
- Are learners supported in setting their own goals, not just receiving externally imposed ones?

**Checkpoint 6.2 — Support Planning and Strategy Development**
- Are checklists, timelines, or planning templates provided?
- Are multi-step tasks broken down with intermediate milestones?

**Checkpoint 6.3 — Facilitate Managing Information and Resources**
- Are resources organized logically and labeled clearly?
- Are learners given tools to track, annotate, or return to information?

**Checkpoint 6.4 — Enhance Capacity for Monitoring Progress**
- Are formative assessments embedded throughout, not just at the end?
- Do learners have access to tools or prompts that help them gauge their own understanding?

## UDL Red Flags — Flag These Before Producing Output

Flag any of the following patterns as UDL violations. They signal assumptions about a default learner that UDL explicitly rejects.

- **Single-format content:** A lecture with no transcript, a PDF with no audio option, a written-only assessment.
- **Single submission format:** "Write a 5-page paper" with no alternatives offered.
- **No background knowledge activation:** Jumping into complex concepts without connecting to prior knowledge.
- **Feedback that is evaluative only:** "You got 70%. Try harder." — with no guidance on what to do next.
- **Engagement that assumes intrinsic motivation:** Content that provides no rationale for why it matters.
- **Assumed language baseline:** Content written for one cultural or linguistic context without consideration of multilingual learners.
- **Time rigidity:** All activities on fixed schedules with no pacing flexibility.
- **Executive function demands without scaffolding:** Multi-step tasks with no planning support.
- **Passive consumption only:** Content the learner watches or reads with no active processing built in.
- **Novelty without transfer:** Concepts introduced but never applied in a new or authentic context.

## UDL Output Self-Check — Run Before Finalizing Any Response

1. **Representation:** Have I presented this information in more than one way, or recommended that the creator do so?
2. **Engagement:** Have I addressed why this content matters and who it matters to?
3. **Action and Expression:** Have I built in or recommended multiple ways for learners to engage and respond?
4. **Flexibility:** Is this design flexible enough to work for a learner with a visual disability, a multilingual learner, a learner with executive function challenges, and a learner with anxiety — simultaneously?
5. **Language:** Have I defined key terms immediately? Have I avoided idioms or culture-specific references that would exclude some learners?
6. **Scaffolding:** Have I provided or recommended enough support for learners who need it without limiting learners who don't?
7. **Feedback:** If feedback is part of this output, is it specific, growth-oriented, and actionable?
8. **Assumptions:** Have I designed for the average learner — or for learner variability?

## UDL and WCAG Together

UDL and WCAG are not the same, but they are deeply compatible. Use them together:

| WCAG Ensures | UDL Ensures |
|---|---|
| Technical access to content | Meaningful engagement with content |
| Screen reader compatibility | Multiple ways to understand information |
| Keyboard navigation | Multiple ways to express understanding |
| Caption accuracy | Comprehension across language and modality |
| Color contrast | Attention and clarity for all learners |

A document can be WCAG-compliant and still fail UDL if it only offers one way to learn. A document can be UDL-rich and still fail WCAG if the technology is inaccessible. Both standards are required. Neither is sufficient alone.
